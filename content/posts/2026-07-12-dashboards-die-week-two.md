Built a dashboard everyone loved in the demo — and nobody opened after week two? It happened to me, and fixing it taught me more about business analysis than any course.

## The demo trap

A dashboard demo is a performance. You narrate the story, you click the right filter at the right moment, and the room nods. But the room isn't nodding at the dashboard — it's nodding at *your narration*. Take the narrator away and the artefact has to speak for itself. Most can't.

## Three questions that save a dashboard

When I designed the Looker dashboards for ISDB operations — the ones that ended up improving decision-making efficiency by 30% — the difference wasn't better charts. It was asking three questions before building anything:

**1. What decision does this screen exist to change?**
Not "what data do we have" — what *decision*. If a chart doesn't move a real decision someone makes weekly, it's decoration. Cut it.

**2. What will the user do in their first 10 seconds?**
People don't explore dashboards; they glance at them. The answer to their most frequent question must be visible without a single click. Everything else is a drill-down.

**3. Who feels pain when this number is wrong?**
If the answer is "nobody", the dashboard has no owner and no future. Find the person whose Monday gets worse when the metric slips — build for them, and name the metric the way *they* name it.

## The week-two test

My rule now: two weeks after launch, pull the usage numbers (yes — a dashboard about the dashboard). If daily opens are falling, the problem is almost never visual polish. It's that question 1 was skipped.

Adoption isn't a launch task. It's a requirements task.
